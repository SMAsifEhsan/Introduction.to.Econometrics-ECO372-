An estimator is a recipe applied to a random sample, so it has its own sampling distribution. Good estimators are unbiased and efficient in small samples, and consistent and asymptotically efficient as the sample grows. Pick a property, and switch the estimator between the sample mean X and the OLS slope β1 to see each idea twice.

How to read these diagrams

Each draw is a fresh random sample. We compute the estimator, drop it into a histogram, and repeat thousands of times — so the histogram is the sampling distribution. The solid vertical line is the true parameter; the dashed line is the average of our estimates. Unbiased means those two coincide; efficient means the histogram is narrow; consistent means it collapses onto the truth as n grows. The dotted bell is the matching theoretical normal.

R² says how much of the variation in test scores the regression accounts for. The total variation splits exactly into an explained part and a residual part: TSS = ESS + RSS, and R² = ESS / TSS. Change the scenario and watch the explained (green) share of the bar grow or shrink.

Common mistake

A high R² does not prove causality. It only says that the fitted line explains a larger share of the variation in the sample. Causal claims need a research design and assumptions, not only a fitted line.

Is STR really related to test scores, or could the estimated slope just be sampling noise? We test H₀: β₁ = 0 against H₁: β₁ ≠ 0. The t-statistic counts how many standard errors β1 sits from 0; if it lands in a shaded tail we reject H₀. Shrink the sample or draw a new one and watch the decision flip.

At the chosen significance level, we reject or fail to reject H₀: β₁ = 0. This means the sample provides enough or not enough statistical evidence that STR is linearly related to test scores. It does not automatically prove the effect is causal or practically large.
